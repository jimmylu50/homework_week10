World Development: The Positive and Negative Consequences

GDP Per Capita is one of the most important indicators which reflects a nation's development in the economic sense.

It is clear that countries such as United States and Australia have the best economic development, which also indicate that their citizens are doing better than most people in other countries.

In contrast, large amount of countries in Africa have very low GDP per capita, showing that they are rather underdeveloped.

On the other hand, general necessity is another means of looking at how different nations have developed over the years.

Basic sanitation is definitely one of the impacting factors which reflect living quality of people. As shown in the line graph on the right, it indicates that there is a steady increase in basic sanitation level across different regions.

It is also clear to see that there is a significant increase in basic sanitation level for underdeveloped regions compared to first-world continents.

As for negative consequences of world development, more CO2 emission had to be made for the improvement of human life quality.

In the interactive scatterplot, we can see that the population and CO2 emission at each nation seem to be somewhat positively correlated.

When we scroll through the past 20 years, it is clear that with more population, more CO2 is emitted.

However, we can see that as we advance through the years, the life expectancy increases in almost all nations due to better life quality.

CO2 is the one of the components that are responsible for global warming and it is produced from burning of fossil fuels, which is frequently done for various uses in human lives.

It is clear that East Asia has seen the greatest increase in CO2 emission across all continents in the world. This is most likely due to the rise of China becoming that world factory and producing more products than ever.

Apart from China, most of the continents have a steady increase of CO2 emission over the past decades, which further imposes the danger of global warming on future generations.

In the interactive scatterplot, it presents correlations between the factors of "Population", "CO2 Emission" and "Number of people in Labor Force".

As shown, there is a strong positive correlation between "Population" and "Labor", meaning that with more people in the society, there are more people in workforce. This can possibly indicate that new jobs are created across various fields.

Similarly, the relationship between "CO2" and "Population" is also somewhat positively correlated.
